## Assessment

### Strengths
1. Ambitious theoretical unification of PA and RLVR under divergence estimation
2. Formal characterization of f-GRPO as divergence estimator with alignment consistency and reward improvement guarantees
3. Canonical link function selection (Table 1) is practically useful guidance
4. Reasonable experimental scope (2 model sizes, 3 math datasets, safety alignment)

### Key Weakness (my focus)
The construction of reward-aligned/unaligned distributions via the truncated advantage criterion (Assumption 1 and Definition 1) is the core conceptual leap the paper makes to extend the divergence framework from PA to RLVR. This construction has a fundamental tension:

- Assumption 1 posits p^±(y|x) ∝ exp(±r(x,y)), but this is introduced as a working assumption for loss motivation and then "weakened" to Assumption 2 (monotone reward-density) for the theorem.
- Definition 1 (the actual reward-aligned distribution used in the theory) adds an indicator term [±(r-μ_r)]_+, which means the aligned distribution has zero mass on below-average responses and the unaligned has zero mass on above-average responses. These distributions are mutually singular by construction.
- Theorem 1 then shows f-GRPO estimates divergence between these distributions, which reduces to the constant f'_∞ (since the distributions are singular). The alignment consistency result (Eq. 22) is essentially a step function: all above-average responses get the same reweighting (exp(β^{-1}g^{-1}(f'_∞))), and below-average get none.

This means f-GRPO, in the infinite-sample limit, reduces to a particularly simple form of reward filtering: it upweights everything above average equally and zeroes out everything below average. The paper argues this is an advantage over GRPO (which retains some mass on below-average), but this advantage comes at the cost of treating all above-average responses identically regardless of how good they are. GRPO's advantage-weighted updates, by contrast, differentiate between slightly-above-average and far-above-average responses.

The math reasoning results (Table 3-4) show modest improvements over GRPO, which is consistent with this mechanism: in domains where discriminating between good and very-good responses matters less (binary correctness reward), equal upweighting of above-average suffices. It's less clear this would generalize to settings with continuous or multi-dimensional rewards.

### Verdict Lean
The paper makes a real theoretical contribution by connecting divergence estimation to on-policy RL. The execution is competent. However, the reward-aligned distribution construction is more of a clever trick than a principled extension, and the empirical gains are modest even in the most favorable setting (binary math rewards). I lean weak reject (~4.0-4.5), primarily because the practical value of the framework over standard GRPO hasn't been convincingly demonstrated, and the strong theoretical claims (maximal reward attainment) rely on a construction that flattens the reward signal into a binary above/below indicator.
