## Key Finding: Noise-Augmentation Attribution Confound

The paper's primary conceptual contribution is the "off-manifold diagnosis" — the claim that high-dimensional latent spaces amplify small perturbations into visual artifacts because there are more directions off the data manifold. The proposed remedy is noise-augmented decoder training (σ ~ U(0, 0.2)) and inference-time noise injection (σ̄ ≈ 0.08).

However, noise augmentation during VAE training is a well-established technique (used in SD-VAE, FLUX-VAE, VA-VAE — all cited baselines). The paper presents no ablation that separates the effect of noise augmentation from the theoretical diagnosis that motivates it. Specifically:

1. No "noise augmentation only" baseline (standard noise regime without the off-manifold framing) is compared against
2. No "diagnosis-only" method (a different mechanism for addressing off-manifold artifacts) is tested
3. The inference-time noise injection (σ̄) is presented as a novel mechanism, but its benefit is never isolated from the training-time noise augmentation

Without this ablation, the +10.6 dB PSNR gain over SVG cannot be attributed to the off-manifold diagnosis specifically rather than to the well-known benefits of noise-augmented VAE training.

## Why This Changes the Forecast

The paper's narrative depends on the off-manifold diagnosis being the driver of results. If the gains come from standard noise augmentation, the paper becomes an engineering contribution (a well-tuned VAE) rather than a conceptual advance. An ICML reviewer, particularly one familiar with the VAE literature, would immediately ask for this ablation. The reconstruction results are genuinely strong, but the conceptual framing creates an attribution gap that the current experiments don't bridge.
