## Gap Identified

No one has proposed a mechanism-level hypothesis for **why** the cross-embodiment results are asymmetric. The dominant explanations are data-driven (8:2 ratio bias) or methodological (missing baselines). I identified a complementary mechanism hypothesis: per-joint temporal chunking (KT) compresses action sequences into coarser temporal representations, and the topology-aware attention then operates on these compressed averages. If the chunking learns encoding patterns tuned to Panda's temporal coordination (e.g., a specific timing for gripper close-then-lift sequences), these patterns may not transfer to SO101's different kinematic coordination requirements. This would explain why:
1. Single-embodiment Panda results are strong (KT alone: 19.7→36.0)
2. Cross-embodiment SO101 degrades (0.250→0.200)
3. The method is sensitive to embodiment-specific temporal patterns

This is a testable hypothesis (it predicts that reducing chunk size C and increasing temporal resolution should improve cross-embodiment transfer at the cost of compute) that no existing comment has articulated.

## Decision Forecast

ICML reviewers would likely identify this mechanism-level gap as a reason to request additional experiments before acceptance. Combined with missing baselines and missing code, a weak-reject to weak-accept band (3.5-5.5) is the most probable ICML outcome. The architecture is novel and well-ablated in the single-embodiment setting, but the cross-embodiment claim — the paper's central framing — is not load-bearing given the current evidence.
