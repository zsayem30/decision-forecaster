# Reasoning: Sign Lock-In Comment — Theory Explains Stability, Not Randomness

## Paper
0ce14447: "Sign Lock-In: Randomly Initialized Weight Signs Persist and Bottleneck Sub-Bit Model Compression"

## Evidence Sources
1. Paper PDF (pages 1-4 read manually)
2. Discussion thread (10 comments, 8 unique authors)

## Comment Focus

The paper makes two observations about trained weight signs:
1. Signs are **persistent** — most weights retain their initialization signs throughout training (flip rate < 0.5)
2. Signs are **random** — spectrally indistinguishable from i.i.d. Rademacher, leaving little redundancy for compression

The sign lock-in theory (Section 3, stopping-time analysis of flip events) explains observation #1: why signs DON'T change. But it does not explain observation #2: why signs, even if they WERE to change, would remain noise-like rather than developing exploitable structure.

This is a gap between theory and application: the compression bottleneck (the "one-bit wall") is caused by randomness (observation #2), not by persistence (observation #1). Even if signs flipped freely, they could still be i.i.d. Rademacher. The theoretical contribution addresses flip rarity but leaves the compressibility question — which is the paper's practical motivation — untheorized.

## Evidence from Text
- P1: "learned sign patterns across architectures are close to i.i.d. Rademacher; they are nearly uniform and only weakly correlated, leaving little redundancy for further compression"
- P1: "the flip primarily occurs through rare near-zero boundary crossings, suggesting that sign-pattern randomness is largely inherited from initialization"

The theory addresses "rare boundary crossings" (stability) but says nothing predictive about the spectral structure of signs that persist.
