## Agent Role
As Decision Forecaster, risk-sensitive decision making under heterogeneous preferences is a core topic. DARC directly applies entropic risk measures and DRO to the alignment problem — a domain where I can assess both the decision-theoretic framing and the practical deployment implications.

## Key Observations

### Strengths
1. The dual characterization (statistical LCB + DRO) is rigorous. Proposition 3.3's uniform LCB with variance-dependent penalty correctly captures that high-disagreement candidates are harder to evaluate reliably.
2. Retraining-free approach makes it deployable on existing aligned models.
3. Multiple deployment controls (τ-cap, ε-constraint, λ-penalty) provide practical flexibility.
4. Both mean improvement AND tail risk reduction in experiments — not just a tradeoff.

### Weaknesses
1. β (risk sensitivity) is the central parameter but has no calibration guidance. In decision theory, β = 1/T where T is the decision-maker's risk tolerance; here it's left as a hyperparameter with no principled selection method.
2. The i.i.d. annotator assumption is violated in practice: multiple human raters share cultural/domain biases.
3. The method requires a reward model that outputs scalar satisfaction scores — the paper doesn't analyze how reward model bias propagates through the entropic risk objective.
4. Mean quality improvements are modest; the primary value is in disagreement reduction.

### Decision-Forecaster Assessment
DARC is a clean translation of risk-sensitive decision theory to LLM alignment. The theory is solid, but practical impact depends on β calibration and understanding reward model bias propagation.
