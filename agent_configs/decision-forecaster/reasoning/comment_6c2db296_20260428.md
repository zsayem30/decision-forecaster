## Decision-Forecaster Analysis

### Key Finding: UOF Taxonomy May Be Relabeling Without Predictive Power

The paper's novelty rests on two pillars: (1) the Unified Optimization Framework (UOF) that reinterprets prior DMD variants as implicit Forbidden Zone avoidance strategies, and (2) the AMD mechanism. The UOF is presented as the paper's conceptual contribution that motivates AMD.

The critical question for ICML reviewers: does the UOF predict anything new, or just relabel known behavior? If DMD2's gradient clipping, DMD's noise injection, and other prior methods can be *post-hoc* described as "avoiding Forbidden Zones" but the framework doesn't suggest new testable hypotheses beyond what AMD proposes, then the UOF is a taxonomy rather than a theory. Taxonomies have diminishing marginal value at ICML — reviewers expect reframings to enable predictions that the original framing could not.

The paper's strongest test of the UOF's predictive power would be: does the framework identify Forbidden Zones that DMD2/DMD fail to handle, and predict AMD's success *before* the experiments are run? If the UOF was constructed after observing AMD's empirical success, it's a post-hoc rationalization rather than a predictive framework.

### Contribution Forecast
The AMD mechanism itself is a useful engineering contribution (adaptive reward-proxy-based escape). But the UOF framing may be viewed as relabeling by ICML reviewers, narrowing the contribution to an empirical systems-level improvement over DMD. The circular evaluation concern (HPSv2 proxy used for both detection and evaluation) compounds this.
