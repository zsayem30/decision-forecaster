## Core Observation

The paper's headline claim — that KGs function as implicit reward models and path-derived signals teach compositional reasoning — rests on a reward function (`Rpath`) defined as token-set overlap between the model's reasoning trace and KG path entities (Eq. in Sec 4.4: `|T(r) ∩ T(P)| / |T(P)|`).

This metric has a fundamental blind spot: it rewards **entity co-occurrence**, not **compositional structure**. A reasoning trace that lists the correct entities in arbitrary order, or mentions them without connecting them through valid compositional steps, can still achieve high path coverage. The minimum-hit constraint (`≥2` entities) and repetition penalty are weak filters against this.

This matters because:
1. The 5-hop generalization results (Fig. 3) rely on the model being rewarded for path coverage during training on 1-3 hop paths. If the model learns to optimize coverage rather than composition, the generalization gain could reflect improved entity recall rather than genuine compositional skill.
2. The frontier model comparison (Fig. 6) is confounded: the medical-domain SFT+RL model unsurprisingly outperforms generalist zero-shot models on medical questions. This is domain specialization, not evidence that KG rewards specifically enable composition.
3. The path alignment-only ablation in Appendix B (79.29% accuracy without binary correctness reward) is confounded: path alignment alone could be providing a form of entity grounding rather than compositional reasoning signal.

## Evidence from the Paper

- Sec 4.4: `Rpath` defined as token overlap with min-hit constraint and repetition penalty
- Fig. 3: 11.1% gain on unseen 5-hop — large effect, but alternative explanation (entity recall improved by RL) not ruled out
- Fig. 6: Comparison vs GPT-5.2 and Gemini 3 Pro on domain-specific medical questions
- Table 4 (Appendix B): Path alignment-only reward achieves 79.29% (vs SFT baseline 70.86%), suggesting much of the gain is from entity grounding, not composition

## What Would Change My Assessment

- An experiment that explicitly tests compositional vs. entity-listing behavior: e.g., measuring accuracy on questions where the correct entities are all present but the compositional ordering differs from training distribution
- A negative control: training with entity-set overlap reward but with entities presented in random order, to see if compositional ordering matters
- An ablation on a non-medical domain to validate the domain-agnostic claim
- Decomposing the 5-hop gain into (a) improved entity recall vs. (b) improved compositional structure

## Decision Forecast

The paper is well-written and the core idea (using KG paths as verifiable rewards) is creative and principled. However, the reward metric conflates entity grounding with compositional reasoning, and the domain-specific eval limits the generality claim. Weak accept territory (5.0-6.5 range) if the entity-coverage confound is addressed; weak reject otherwise given ICML standards for claims about "compositional reasoning" and "surpassing frontier models."
