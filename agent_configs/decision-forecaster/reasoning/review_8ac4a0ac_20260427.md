## Core Observation: Binary rins Dominates GRPO Advantage

### The Mechanism

The paper defines the total reward as three additive components:

**Eq 5 (main text):** `ri = λ1 · sim(Φsig(Vi), Ψsig(Tq)) + λ2 · I(rules(oi, q))`

**Algorithm 1 pseudocode (line 13):** `ri = rsem + rins + rkn`

**Inconsistency:** Eq 5 only includes rsem and rins, omitting rkn. The pseudocode uses simple unweighted sum of all three. The λ weights are never specified with numeric values anywhere in the paper.

### Why This Matters

In GRPO, the advantage for sample i is: `Âi = (ri - mean(r)) / std(r)`

This normalization means the contribution of each reward component to the gradient is proportional to its variance relative to the group, not its magnitude:

- **rins** is binary (0 or 1). When roughly half the G=8 group satisfies rules and half doesn't, rins contributes ~0.25 to the variance -- the maximum possible for a [0,1] binary.
- **rsem** is continuous cosine similarity, typically in [0.2, 0.8]. For a group of independently generated images, SigLIP similarity scores often cluster tightly (e.g., all between 0.55 and 0.65), contributing σ² ≈ 0.001 to the variance.
- **rkn** is VQA confidence, also continuous in [0,1], with similar low-variance characteristics.

This means rins can contribute **100-250x more** to std(r) than rsem, making the normalized advantage almost entirely a function of rule satisfaction. The model primarily learns to generate images that satisfy verifiable constraints (aspect ratio, object count, text presence), while the SigLIP semantic signal is effectively reduced to noise in the gradient.

### Corroborating Evidence from the Paper

1. **Figure 2:** "rsem converges the fastest" -- this is suspicious under the variance-dominance hypothesis. If rsem provides near-zero normalized advantage, its fast convergence suggests the metric itself saturates trivially (all samples get similar SigLIP scores) rather than reflecting genuine semantic improvement.

2. **Appendix E.1 (Spatial Reasoning):** "We attribute LVRPO's success here to the Instruction-Following Reward (rins). By explicitly verifying spatial constraints (e.g., bounding box intersection checks during reward calculation)..." -- the authors themselves attribute the strongest qualitative result to rins, not rsem.

3. **The missing decomposition ablation:** The paper only ablates "rsem+rins" vs "rsem+rins+rkn" (Figure 3) but never isolates rsem-only, rins-only, or rkn-only conditions. A rsem-only ablation would reveal whether SigLIP delivers any independent benefit, or whether rins accounts for essentially all gains.

4. **D.2 Scaling note:** "The 1.3B model failed to effectively leverage the complex logical constraints (rins)" -- this confirms rins carries the difficulty, while rsem is learnable even at small scale.

### ICML Relevance

For an ICML submission that frames its contribution as "semantic alignment via GRPO preference optimization," the paper needs to demonstrate that the semantic component (rsem) independently contributes to the observed improvements. The current evidence is equally consistent with "rule-based filtering via GRPO" as the mechanism, with rsem serving as a weak regularizer. Without the per-component decomposition ablation, the paper's central claim about semantic grounding is unverifiable.

## Conclusion

The paper's empirical gains are real and the idea of applying GRPO to unified multimodal models is reasonable. But the evidence that the gains come from the claimed mechanism (semantic grounding via SigLIP) rather than from rule-based filtering (rins) is insufficient. I would put this in the weak accept range if the reward decomposition data can be provided; weak reject if not.
